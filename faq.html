Pizza dough questions, answered straight

The most common things that go wrong with pizza dough — and why. No filler, just the mechanism. When you're ready to build a recipe, the calculator handles the maths.

Short answerYes — any plain flour works, as long as it's not self-raising or cake flour.

Different flours have different properties, and the one that matters most for bread and pizza is protein content, usually printed on the packet as a percentage. Most plain flours sit between about 9% and 11%; anything above 12% is considered a strong flour.

More protein means a stronger, chewier structure. Weaker flour gives a softer, more tender crumb. If your flour is on the weak side, you can firm up its gluten by adding a little vinegar — the calculator does this automatically when you tell it your protein percentage.

Short answerAlmost always too much water, or not enough kneading. Keep kneading to find out which.

Stickiness comes from a few factors working together, but if we set the flour's protein aside and just work with what you've got, the two main culprits are too much water and not enough kneading.

The only way to tell them apart is to keep kneading. If the dough is still sticky after about 20 minutes, it's likely too wet. From there you have two options. If you want to keep your hydration where it is, work a little oil into the dough. If you'd rather not add oil, add a little flour instead — but be aware this lowers your hydration.

Short answerThere's no fixed time — judge by size. Let the dough roughly double during bulk ferment, and let the balls roughly double during the final proof.

Time isn't something you set; it's something that emerges once everything else is decided. Fermentation depends on ambient temperature, hydration, yeast quantity, salt, any added sugar, protein content and how long you kneaded. Only after all of that is baked into the dough does a time reveal itself.

The matrix, roughly: higher temperature means less time, higher hydration means less time, more yeast means less time, more salt means more time, more sugar means less time, higher protein means more time, and more kneading means less resting time.

Because of all that, the easiest approach is to treat the clock as a guide and watch the dough instead — let it at least double for both stages. When every factor favours speed, each stage can be done in under two hours. When the factors favour a long, slow rise, it can stretch to 48 hours or more.

Short answerThere are several possible reasons, but the most practical move is usually to start again.

A dough that won't rise is a fault-finding exercise. Assuming your yeast is alive and able to activate, the usual culprit is that the dough got too hot and killed the yeast — most yeast strains start to die above 40°C, and dough temperature climbs surprisingly fast from the friction of long kneading.

Other common causes: forgetting to add the yeast, not checking it was still alive first, temperatures too cold to get going, too much salt, or not enough water.

Short answerYes and no — you can make something pizza-like, but nothing matches a classic yeasted dough.

There are workarounds, but none of them replace yeast, so the honest answer is: you can make something that resembles a pizza, but you can't make a classic-style pizza without yeast.

You can use self-raising flour, add eggs for extra protein, or reach for dough conditioners like xanthan or guar gum — but none of these do what yeast does. Yeast doesn't just make gas; it works the dough at a microscopic level, developing the gluten in a way nothing else can. If you don't believe it, try it side by side.

And if the real problem is that you simply can't get hold of yeast, then with a little patience you can make your own — see our yeast tips for more.

Short answerTearing comes from stretching too fast, dough that isn't ready, dough that's too cold, or low hydration and protein. Even perfect dough tears if you're rough with it.

Before you stretch, the dough has to be ready to stretch — and even then, being too rough will tear it. Setting that aside, the main cause of tearing is underdeveloped gluten, which usually comes down to too little hydration, too little kneading, or both. Cold dough tears too.

At the other extreme, dough can actually be over-kneaded. A dough should be fully kneaded in under 30 minutes of total kneading. If you're going longer than that, something in the recipe needs another look — it may be too wet, too dry or too salty. Past about 30 minutes you start shortening the long gluten strands instead of lengthening them, and by around 40 minutes the dough turns to mush. The sweet spot is roughly 10–15 minutes by machine, or 15–20 minutes by hand.

Dough can be frozen at any stage of its development. When it thaws, it simply picks up where it left off and resumes developing.

Short answerIt depends mainly on thickness. Thin and hot (around 400°C); thick like Detroit, cooler (around 250°C).

Neapolitan wants the most heat to puff that cornicione — around 430°C. New York likes it hot too, around 350°C. These thinner pizzas do best cooked hot and fast, in roughly 90 to 180 seconds.

Thicker pizzas like Detroit and its cousin focaccia take longer to cook through, so they need less heat on the outside to stop the surface burning before the centre is done — around 250°C and 220°C respectively, taking anywhere from about 10 to 20 minutes.

Short answerNot cooked hot or long enough, too wet going in, moisture trapped under the base, sauce leaking through, or the pizza resting on a flat surface afterwards.

A soggy base is usually a combination of three things: not enough heat, not enough direct heat under the base, and nowhere for the moisture to escape during and after cooking. Baking on a tray traps steam underneath; baking on stone or steel lets it cook out. And once it's done, resting it flat on a board lets the trapped steam soften it again.

Short answerA domestic oven with a thick pizza steel is the best option.

A home oven can absolutely produce a crispy base, but it needs help. The best option is a thick pizza steel — let it preheat fully, then drop the pizza straight onto it with no tray in between.

If you don't have a steel, there's one more option, though it's fiddly. Blind-bake the base first, flip it over and bake the empty base upside down for about 5 minutes, then top it and bake again. When it comes out, rest it on a wire rack so the bottom stays crisp.
