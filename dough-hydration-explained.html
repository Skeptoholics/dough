Troubleshooting

Why is my pizza dough not rising?

Dough that won't rise is almost always caused by dead yeast, water that was too hot, a too-cold room, or simply not enough time. A cold room is the most common cause — and the easiest to fix.

A flat, dense dough is disheartening, but the cause is usually mundane. Work through these in order.

1. Your kitchen is too cold

This is the number-one culprit. Yeast slows dramatically as temperature drops — in a cool room a dough can take far longer than the recipe says. The dough may be fine; it just needs more time or a warmer spot (near a warm oven, or in the oven with just the light on).

2. The water killed the yeast

Water above about 50 °C starts killing yeast; hotter still and it's dead on contact. If you used "warm" water that was actually hot, the yeast is gone. Water should feel cool to lukewarm, never hot. Nothing fixes this but starting over.

3. The yeast was already dead

Old or badly-stored yeast loses potency. To test it: dissolve a little in warm water with a pinch of sugar and wait about 10 minutes. If it doesn't foam, it's dead — replace it.

4. You haven't waited long enough

With the low yeast quantities good dough uses, rising is slow by design. A long ferment can look like nothing's happening for hours, then move quickly. Judge by how the dough looks — puffy, domed, jiggly — not by the clock.

Avoid it next time

Most "won't rise" problems are really "wrong yeast for the temperature." Give Doughborough your room temperature and it sets the dose and a realistic timing window, so you know whether to wait or start again.
